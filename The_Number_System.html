1 · Rational numbers & their decimals

A number is rational if it can be written as a fraction a/b (with b ≠ 0). That includes every integer, fraction, and any decimal that ends or repeats.

By the end: you can convert a fraction to its decimal and tell whether that decimal terminates or repeats.

What makes a number rational

Divide the numerator by the denominator. Every rational number's decimal does one of two things:

  • Terminates (ends): 3/8 = 0.375
  • Repeats forever — a single digit or a block of digits repeating without end: 2/3 = 0.6666…
Worked example

5/6: divide 5 by 6 → 0.8333… The 3 repeats forever, so it is rational with a repeating decimal.

Shortcut: a fraction in lowest terms terminates exactly when the only prime factors of its denominator are 2 and/or 5 (like 2, 4, 5, 8, 10, 20). Otherwise it repeats.
Big picture: the rational numbers and the irrational numbers together make up the real numbers — every point on the number line is one of them.

2 · Repeating decimals → fractions

Because repeating decimals are rational, each one equals an exact fraction. Here is the standard trick to find it.

By the end: you can turn a repeating decimal into a fraction in lowest terms.

The “multiply and subtract” method

Step 1: let x equal the decimal. Step 2: multiply by a power of 10 so the repeating blocks line up. Step 3: subtract to cancel the repeat, then solve for x.

Worked example

Convert 0.4444… Let x = 0.4444…, so 10x = 4.4444… Subtract: 10x − x = 4, giving 9x = 4 and x = 4/9.

Worked example · repeats eventually

Convert 0.6333… (only the 3 repeats). Let x = 0.6333… Then 10x = 6.333… and 100x = 63.33… Subtract: 100x − 10x = 57, so 90x = 57 and x = 57/90 = 19/30.

Pattern: one repeating digit goes over 9 (0.7777… = 7/9); two repeating digits over 99 (0.2727… = 3/11). If a non-repeating digit comes first, multiply by both powers of 10 and subtract.

3 · Irrational numbers

Some numbers are not rational. Their decimals go on forever without ever repeating, so they can never be written as a fraction.

By the end: you can decide whether a number is rational or irrational.

What counts as irrational

An irrational number cannot be written as a fraction of integers; its decimal never ends and never repeats.

  • π = 3.14159… and √2 = 1.41421… are irrational.
  • The square root of a number that is not a perfect square is irrational: √10, √7.
  • But the square root of a perfect square is rational: √16 = 4, √100 = 10.
Worked example

√36 = 6 → rational (6 is a fraction: 6/1). √5 = 2.2360… never repeats → irrational.

4 · Square roots & cube roots

A square root undoes squaring; a cube root undoes cubing. Knowing the perfect squares and cubes by sight makes the rest of this unit fast.

By the end: you can evaluate square and cube roots of perfect squares and cubes.

Roots of perfect squares and cubes

√n asks “what number, squared, gives n?” and ∛n asks “what number, cubed, gives n?”

Perfect squares: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100, 121, 144, 169, 196, 225.
Perfect cubes: 1, 8, 27, 64, 125.

Worked example

√144 = 12 because 12² = 144.   ∛27 = 3 because 3³ = 27.

5 · Approximating irrational numbers

When a root is not a perfect square, you can still pin down its value by trapping it between perfect squares you know.

By the end: you can estimate an irrational square root and place it between whole numbers.

Trap it between perfect squares

Find the perfect squares just below and just above. The root lies between their roots.

Worked example

Estimate √20. Since 16 < 20 < 25, we get 4 < √20 < 5. Testing: 4.4² = 19.36 and 4.5² = 20.25, so √20 ≈ 4.47.

Truncating: this is how √2 is built — between 1 and 2, then 1.4 and 1.5, then 1.41 and 1.42, getting more exact each step.

6 · Comparing, ordering & estimating expressions

Once you can approximate irrational numbers, you can compare them, order them on a number line, and estimate expressions that contain them.

By the end: you can compare rationals and irrationals and estimate the value of an expression like π².

Approximate, then compare

Replace each irrational number with a decimal estimate, then compare. To order several numbers, plot each estimate on a number line — reading left to right gives least to greatest.

Handy values: π ≈ 3.14, √2 ≈ 1.41, √10 ≈ 3.16.

Worked example

Order √8, 3, π from least to greatest. Estimate: √8 ≈ 2.83, then 3, then π ≈ 3.14. So √8 < 3 < π.

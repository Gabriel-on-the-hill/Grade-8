1 · Atoms, Elements, Molecules & Compounds

All matter is built from atoms. How they combine decides what a substance is.

By the end: you can read a chemical formula and tell atoms, elements, molecules and compounds apart.

The building blocks of matter

Everything is made of atoms. An element is a substance made of only one kind of atom (hydrogen, oxygen, carbon, gold). When two or more atoms bond, they form a molecule. A compound is a molecule made of two or more different elements.

A chemical formula names the elements and counts the atoms: the small subscript tells how many atoms of the element before it. In water, H₂O, there are 2 hydrogen atoms and 1 oxygen atom.

A pure substance is made of one kind of particle (water, copper). A mixture is two or more substances physically together, not chemically bonded (salt water, air). Some substances stay as separate simple molecules; others repeat a unit over and over into an extended structure.

Worked example

Glucose is C₆H₁₂O₆. Count each element: 6 carbon + 12 hydrogen + 6 oxygen = 24 atoms in one molecule.

Read it this way: a letter with no subscript means one atom (the O in H₂O).
LearnElement or compound?
Which of these is a compound?
A compound contains two or more different elements chemically bonded. Water is hydrogen + oxygen. O₂, C and Au are each a single element.
GuidedCount the atoms
Methane is CH₄. How many atoms are in one methane molecule in total?
PracticePure substance or mixture?
Select every pure substance.
A pure substance is one kind of particle. If it can be physically separated into different parts, it is a mixture.

2 · States of Matter & the Particle Model

The same particles can be a solid, a liquid or a gas — what changes is how they are arranged and how they move.

By the end: you can use the particle model to explain the properties of solids, liquids and gases.

Solids, liquids and gases — at the particle level

  • Solid: particles packed closely in a fixed arrangement, vibrating in place — definite shape and volume.
  • Liquid: particles still close but able to slide past each other — definite volume, takes the shape of its container.
  • Gas: particles far apart, moving fast and freely — spreads to fill its whole container.

The particles themselves never change — only their spacing, arrangement and motion.

Worked example

Why does ice float? In most substances the solid is denser than the liquid, but when water freezes its molecules lock into an open pattern that spreads them slightly farther apart. The same mass fills more space, so ice is less dense than liquid water and floats.

LearnName the state
In which state are the particles far apart and moving freely, spreading to fill any container?
In a gas the particles have the most energy and space, so they spread to fill the whole container.
PracticeRead the properties
A sample keeps the same volume but takes the shape of whatever container it is poured into. Which state is it?
Liquid particles stay close together (fixed volume) but slide past one another (no fixed shape).
ApplyExplain with the model
Ice floats on liquid water. Using the particle model, this is because in ice the water molecules are —
Freezing arranges water molecules into an open pattern that spreads them apart, so ice is less dense and floats.

3 · Thermal Energy & Changes of State

Adding or removing thermal energy changes how fast particles move — and can change the state of a substance.

By the end: you can predict how particle motion, temperature and state change as thermal energy is added or removed, and name each phase change.

Energy in, energy out

Adding thermal energy makes particles move faster; temperature measures the average motion (kinetic energy) of the particles. Add enough energy and the particles break free of their arrangement — the substance changes state. Remove energy and they slow down and draw closer.

The changes of state: melting (solid→liquid), freezing (liquid→solid), boiling / vaporizing (liquid→gas), condensation (gas→liquid), and sublimation (solid→gas). A change of state is a physical change — the substance stays the same substance.

Worked example

Heat ice: the molecules vibrate faster until, at 0°C, they break the fixed arrangement and melt to liquid. Keep heating: at 100°C they move fast enough to escape and boil to gas. Through it all it is still H₂O — the mass and identity never change.

LearnName the change of state
The change of state from a gas to a liquid is called ___.
GuidedPredict the particles
You remove thermal energy from liquid water. Its particles —
Removing thermal energy lowers particle motion; the particles slow and draw closer, and can freeze into a solid.

4 · Physical & Chemical Changes

Some changes only rearrange a substance; others make a brand-new substance. Learning to tell them apart is a core skill.

By the end: you can classify a change as physical or chemical, use properties to identify a substance, and spot the evidence of a chemical reaction.

Same substance, or new substance?

A physical change alters form but not identity — no new substance is made (melting, dissolving, tearing, changing state). A chemical change (a chemical reaction) forms one or more new substances with new properties.

Physical properties — density, melting/boiling point, solubility, color, hardness — help identify a substance without changing it. Evidence a chemical reaction happened: a color change, gas bubbles (not from boiling), a temperature change you did not cause, light or an odor produced, or a solid precipitate forming — and the change is usually hard to reverse.

Worked example

Melting an ice cube vs. burning a log. The melted ice is still water — a physical change. The burning log gives off light and heat and leaves ash and smoke (new substances) — a chemical change.

LearnPhysical or chemical?
Which of these is a chemical change?
Rusting forms a new substance (iron oxide). Melting, dissolving and crushing change only the form — the substance stays the same.
PracticeEvidence of a reaction
Select every observation that is evidence a chemical reaction has occurred.
Look for a NEW substance or an energy change you did not cause. Melting and dissolving are physical — nothing new is made.
ApplyUse a property to identify
One clear liquid boils at 100°C; another clear liquid boils at 78°C. Boiling point is a property that identifies a substance. The best conclusion is —
A characteristic property such as boiling point identifies a substance. Different boiling points mean different substances.

5 · Conservation of Mass

In a chemical reaction atoms are only rearranged — never created or destroyed. That single idea lets you predict masses.

By the end: you can apply conservation of mass to find an unknown mass and explain an apparent mass change.

Atoms are rearranged, never lost

During a chemical reaction, the atoms in the reactants come apart and recombine into the products. Every atom is still there afterward — so the total number of each kind of atom, and the total mass, is the same before and after.

In a closed system: mass of reactants = mass of products. If a reaction seems to lose mass, a gas escaped from an open container — weigh the gas too and the mass is unchanged.

Worked example

In a sealed flask, 4 g of hydrogen reacts with 32 g of oxygen to make water. No atoms are lost, so the water produced has mass 4 g + 32 g = 36 g.

LearnFind the product mass
In a sealed flask, 12 g of carbon reacts completely with 32 g of oxygen to form carbon dioxide. What is the mass of the carbon dioxide, in grams?
Atoms are only rearranged, never lost, so total mass before equals total mass after. Add the two masses you are given.
GuidedWhere did the mass go?
A candle burns down and the leftover wax weighs less than the original candle. How is mass still conserved?
In an open container the gas products drift away. Weigh everything — including the escaping gases — and the mass is unchanged.
PracticeWork backward
In a closed system, 36 g of water is broken down into hydrogen gas and 32 g of oxygen gas. What mass of hydrogen forms, in grams?
ReasoningExplain the reading
A student mixes baking soda and vinegar in an open cup on a scale. During the reaction the scale reading goes down. Explain, using conservation of mass, why the mass appears to decrease even though no atoms were destroyed.
ExamMISA · conservation of mass (MS-PS1-5)
A student mixes baking soda and vinegar inside a sealed, rigid container and measures the total mass before and after. The mixture fizzes and bubbles form, and the total mass afterwards is the same as before. The student repeats the investigation in an open container and finds the total mass afterwards is less than before. Which statement best explains both results?
The same atoms exist before and after a chemical reaction, so the total mass cannot change. Sealed, the gas is trapped and still on the balance. Open, the gas leaves — so what remains weighs less. The mass was not destroyed; it left the container.

6 · Synthetic Materials & Energy in Reactions

Reactions build the materials we use and move energy in or out — the science behind a hand-warmer and a cold pack.

By the end: you can describe where synthetic materials come from, classify reactions as endothermic or exothermic, and judge a design's criteria and constraints.

Making materials, moving energy

Synthetic materials — plastics, medicines, fuels — are manufactured by chemically processing natural resources (crude oil, ores, plants). They bring benefits and impacts (cost, waste, pollution): a design trade-off.

Reactions also transfer thermal energy. An exothermic reaction releases energy and warms its surroundings (a hand-warmer). An endothermic reaction absorbs energy and cools its surroundings (an instant cold pack). Engineers define the criteria (what it must do) and constraints (limits it must respect), then test and improve the design.

Worked example

An instant cold pack: squeezing it breaks an inner pouch, a salt dissolves, and the process absorbs thermal energy from its surroundings — so the pack turns cold. That is an endothermic change.

LearnEnergy out
A reaction that releases thermal energy and warms its surroundings is —
Exothermic means energy exits the reaction into the surroundings, so they warm up — the way a hand-warmer works.
PracticeEnergy in
An instant cold pack turns cold right after you squeeze it. The reaction inside must be —
Endothermic reactions pull thermal energy in, so the surroundings — and the pack — get colder.
ApplyJudge the design
Engineers design a hand-warmer for hikers. Which of these are sensible criteria or constraints for the design? Select all that apply.
A criterion states what the design must DO or how it keeps the user safe. Making something cost as much as possible is never a design goal.
PracticeWhere materials come from
Plastic is a synthetic material. Where do its raw materials originally come from?
Synthetic materials are manufactured by chemically processing natural resources; most plastics start from crude oil.

Phenomenon: the Water and Sugar investigation

Students placed 50 mL of water (H₂O) and 50 mL of table sugar (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁) at 0°C, 20°C and 100°C. The water was solid at 0°C (taking up more than 50 mL), liquid at 20°C, and boiling at 100°C. The sugar stayed a solid at all three temperatures. The questions below are real released MISA items built on this phenomenon.

ExamMISA · structure of table sugar
Table sugar (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁) is composed of —
A chemical formula lists the elements that make up the substance. C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ is built from carbon, hydrogen and oxygen atoms.
ExamMISA · why water is a molecule
Water is classified as a molecule because it —
A molecule is two or more atoms chemically bonded together — for water, two hydrogen atoms bonded to one oxygen atom.
ExamMISA · sugar vs. water
How do table sugar molecules differ from water molecules?
Table sugar can link its molecular units into extended, repeating structures; water exists as separate simple molecules.
ExamMISA · freezing and volume
The volume of water increases when water freezes because —
Freezing rearranges the molecules into a more open pattern that takes up more space — the atoms and their mass do not change.
ExamMISA · the thermometer (two-part)
Part A · In the thermometer model, the liquid expanded because the ___ of the liquid’s molecules changes…
Adding thermal energy speeds up particle motion, so the molecules spread apart and the liquid rises up the tube.
Part B · …changes proportionally to the change in ___.
Particle motion increases in step with temperature — hotter means faster-moving particles.
ExamMISA · constructed response
Use evidence from the Water and Sugar investigation to explain what causes water to change phases. Be sure to include: (1) the transfer of thermal energy, and (2) what happens to the arrangement and motion of the water molecules.

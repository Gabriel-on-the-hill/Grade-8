1 · Properties of integer exponents

Exponents are repeated multiplication. A handful of properties let you rewrite and simplify powers quickly.

By the end: you can apply the product, quotient, power, zero, and negative exponent properties.

The exponent properties

  • Product of Powers: am · an = am+n
  • Quotient of Powers: am ÷ an = am−n
  • Power of a Power: (am)n = am·n
  • Zero Exponent: a0 = 1
  • Negative Exponent: a−n = 1/an
Worked example

32 · 34 = 36 (add exponents). (52)3 = 56 (multiply). 2−3 = 1/23 = 1/8.

2 · Scientific notation

Scientific notation writes very large or very small numbers compactly as a number times a power of 10.

By the end: you can convert to and from scientific notation and operate with it.

The form a × 10n

The coefficient a satisfies 1 ≤ a < 10. A positive n means a large number; a negative n means a small one.

Worked example

4.2 × 103 = 4200.   0.00056 = 5.6 × 10−4.   To multiply: (3 × 105)(2 × 102) = 6 × 107 (multiply coefficients, add exponents).

3 · Proportional relationships & slope

Slope measures steepness as a constant rate of change. For a proportional relationship, that rate is the unit rate.

By the end: you can find slope and read slope and y-intercept from y = mx + b.

Slope and the equation of a line

slope = rise/run = (change in y)/(change in x). A proportional relationship is y = mx (through the origin); a general line is y = mx + b, where m is the slope and b is the y-intercept.

Worked example

A line through (0,0) and (2,6) has slope 6/2 = 3, so y = 3x. A line with slope 2 and y-intercept 5 is y = 2x + 5.

4 · Solving linear equations

Solve by distributing, combining like terms, and getting the variable alone — always doing the same thing to both sides.

By the end: you can solve multi-step equations, including ones with variables on both sides.

A reliable order of moves

Distribute → combine like terms → collect variables on one side → isolate the variable.

Worked example

2(x + 3) = 4x − 2 → 2x + 6 = 4x − 2 → 8 = 2x → x = 4.

5 · One, none, or infinitely many solutions

Not every equation has exactly one answer. How the variables behave tells you how many solutions there are.

By the end: you can decide whether a linear equation has one, no, or infinitely many solutions.

Read the result

  • One solution: you reach x = a number (e.g. 2x = 10).
  • No solution: the variables cancel and you get a false statement (e.g. 3 = 5).
  • Infinitely many: the variables cancel and you get a true statement (e.g. 6 = 6).
Worked example

3(x + 2) = 3x + 6 → 3x + 6 = 3x + 6 → 6 = 6 (always true) → infinitely many solutions.

LearnHow many?
Q18 · How many solutions does 2x + 1 = 2x + 1 have?
Both sides are identical — true for every x, so infinitely many solutions.

6 · Systems of linear equations

A system is two equations at once. Its solution is the single point that makes both true — where the lines cross.

By the end: you can solve a system and explain what the solution means.

The solution is the intersection

Solve by graphing (find where the lines meet) or by substitution (set the expressions equal). Parallel lines → no solution; the same line → infinitely many.

Worked example

y = x + 1 and y = 2x − 1: set equal, x + 1 = 2x − 1 → x = 2, then y = 3. Solution: (2, 3).

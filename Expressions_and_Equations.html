1 · Properties of integer exponents

Exponents are repeated multiplication. A handful of properties let you rewrite and simplify powers quickly.

By the end: you can apply the product, quotient, power, zero, and negative exponent properties.

The exponent properties

  • Product of Powers: am · an = am + n
  • Quotient of Powers: am ÷ an = am−n
  • Power of a Power: (am)n = am·n
  • Zero Exponent: a0 = 1
  • Negative Exponent: a−n = 1/an
Worked example

32 · 34 = 36 (add exponents). (52)3 = 56 (multiply). 2−3 = 1/23 = 1/8.

2 · Scientific notation

Scientific notation writes very large or very small numbers compactly as a number times a power of 10.

By the end: you can convert to and from scientific notation and operate with it.

The form a × 10n

The coefficient a satisfies 1 ≤ a < 10. A positive n means a large number; a negative n means a small one.

Worked example

4.2 × 103 = 4200.   0.00056 = 5.6 × 10−4.   To multiply: (3 × 105)(2 × 102) = 6 × 107 (multiply coefficients, add exponents).

3 · Proportional relationships & slope

Slope measures steepness as a constant rate of change. For a proportional relationship, that rate is the unit rate.

By the end: you can find slope and read slope and y-intercept from y = mx + b.

Slope and the equation of a line

slope = rise/run = (change in y)/(change in x). A proportional relationship is y = mx (through the origin); a general line is y = mx + b, where m is the slope and b is the y-intercept.

Worked example

A line through (0,0) and (2,6) has slope 6/2 = 3, so y = 3x. A line with slope 2 and y-intercept 5 is y = 2x + 5.

Why one number describes the whole line

Pick any two points on a non-vertical line and work out (vertical change) ÷ (horizontal change). You get the same ratio every time. That is why a single number — the slope — can describe the whole line, and it is what makes y = mx and y = mx + b work at all.

Worked example

The line through (0, 0), (2, 3), (6, 9) and (10, 15):

From (0, 0) to (2, 3) → 3 ÷ 2.
From (2, 3) to (6, 9) → 6 ÷ 4, and 6 ÷ 4 = 3 ÷ 2.
From (6, 9) to (10, 15) → 6 ÷ 4 = 3 ÷ 2 again.

Three different pairs of points, one ratio. The slope is 3/2.

Use whichever two points are easiest to read. Because the ratio never changes, you are free to pick the pair that lands on grid corners. (Older texts derive this with similar triangles; MCCRS 2025 asks for the equivalent-ratios argument above.)

ApplyWhy the slope does not depend on the points
Q7 · Why does a non-vertical line have just one slope, whichever two of its points you use?
Equivalent ratios are the whole reason. If two pairs of points gave different ratios the graph would bend, so it would not be a straight line. (Note that the second option is false anyway — y = mx + b misses the origin whenever b is not 0.)
ExamMCAP · 8.EE.B.6

Triangle PRT and triangle QRS are shown on the coordinate plane. Points P, Q, and R all lie on the same line, and points T, S, and R all lie on the y-axis. The vertices of both triangles have integer coordinates.

Which statement about the slopes is true?
Both triangles have the same line as their hypotenuse, so both give the same slope. Take rise over run in each: TR/TP = 10 / 6 = 5 / 3 and SR/SQ = 5 / 3. Equal ratios, equal slope — which is exactly the argument in the teach card above. The tempting wrong answer pairs a run ratio with a rise ratio.

4 · Solving linear equations

Solve by distributing, combining like terms, and getting the variable alone — always doing the same thing to both sides.

By the end: you can solve multi-step equations, including ones with variables on both sides.

A reliable order of moves

Distribute → combine like terms → collect variables on one side → isolate the variable.

Worked example

2(x + 3) = 4x − 2 → 2x + 6 = 4x − 2 → 8 = 2x → x = 4.

5 · One, none, or infinitely many solutions

Not every equation has exactly one answer. How the variables behave tells you how many solutions there are.

By the end: you can decide whether a linear equation has one, no, or infinitely many solutions.

Read the result

  • One solution: you reach x = a number (e.g. 2x = 10).
  • No solution: the variables cancel and you get a false statement (e.g. 3 = 5).
  • Infinitely many: the variables cancel and you get a true statement (e.g. 6 = 6).
Worked example

3(x + 2) = 3x + 6 → 3x + 6 = 3x + 6 → 6 = 6 (always true) → infinitely many solutions.

LearnHow many?
Q21 · How many solutions does 2x + 1 = 2x + 1 have?
Both sides are identical — true for every x, so infinitely many solutions.
PracticeHow many?
Q22 · How many solutions does x + 4 = x − 4 have?
Subtract x from both sides: 4 = −4, which is false — no solution.
PracticeMulti-select
Q23 · Select every equation that has exactly one solution.
If the variable terms cancel, it's none or infinitely many. If a variable survives, it's exactly one.
ReasoningConstructed-response (teacher-reviewed)

ExamExam-style · 8.EE.C.7
Q25 · A student solves 3(x + 2) = 3x + 6, reaches 6 = 6, and concludes the equation has no solution. Which statement best describes the error?
When the variable cancels, read what is left. A true statement such as 6 = 6 means both sides were the same expression all along, so every x works. A false statement such as 6 = 5 is the one that means no solution.

6 · Systems of linear equations

A system is two equations at once. Its solution is the single point that makes both true — where the lines cross.

By the end: you can solve a system and explain what the solution means.

The solution is the intersection

Solve by graphing (find where the lines meet) or by substitution (set the expressions equal). Parallel lines → no solution; the same line → infinitely many.

Worked example

y = x + 1 and y = 2x − 1: set equal, x + 1 = 2x − 1 → x = 2, then y = 3. Solution: (2, 3).

ExamMCAP · 8.EE.C.8

The graph of the system of equations y = 2x + 3 and y = x + 5 is shown on the coordinate plane.

Part A · The solution to the system is …
The two lines meet at (2, 7). Check it algebraically: 2x + 3 = x + 5 gives x = 2, and then y = 2(2) + 3 = 7.
Part B · … because the solution to the system must …
A point on both lines is a point that makes both equations true at once. That is what the intersection of the two graphs means, and it is why a system can have no solution (parallel lines) or infinitely many (the same line twice).

8 · Linear inequalities

Solving is almost identical to an equation — but the answer is a range, and one move flips the sign.

By the end: you can solve a linear inequality, show its solution set on a number line, and say what the range means in context.

A range of answers, not one answer

An equation usually has a single solution; an inequality usually has a whole range of them. Solve it exactly as you solve an equation — undo the operations in reverse — with one extra rule: if you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number, the inequality sign turns round.

Worked example

3x + 5 > 20
Subtract 5 from both sides → 3x > 15
Divide both sides by 3 → x > 5

Every number above 5 is a solution: 5.1, 6, 100. The number 5 itself is not, because 3(5) + 5 = 20, and 20 is not greater than 20.

On a number line: an open circle for < or > (the boundary is excluded), a closed circle for ≤ or ≥ (it is included). Then shade the side that satisfies the inequality.
GuidedSolve a two-step inequality

Add 7 to both sides → 4x ≤ 24, then divide by 4 → x ≤ 6. Dividing by positive 4 leaves the sign alone, and ≤ stays ≤.
